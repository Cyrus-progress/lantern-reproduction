Part 01 · The Problem

Tiny fat bubbles that carry medicine

To work, an mRNA vaccine has to get its instructions inside your cells. It hitches a ride in a microscopic bubble of fat. Some bubbles are great couriers; most are terrible.

High School

mRNA is a set of instructions that tells your cells what to build (for a vaccine, a harmless piece of a virus so your body learns to fight it). But mRNA is fragile and cells don't let strangers in. So scientists wrap it in a tiny fat bubble — a lipid nanoparticle — that smuggles it inside.

How good a bubble is at delivering its cargo is called its transfection efficiency. High = great delivery. The problem: there are millions of possible fat molecules, and testing each one in a lab is slow and expensive. The dream is a computer that can just predict the score from the molecule's design.

Undergrad

Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) encapsulate mRNA and mediate its delivery into the cytosol. The ionizable lipid component dominates delivery performance, and small changes to its structure swing transfection efficiency by orders of magnitude.

Wet-lab screening is low-throughput and costly, which motivates a structure–activity (QSAR-style) machine-learning model: feed in the molecule's structure, predict the efficiency, and screen millions of candidates in silico before synthesizing any.

PhD

The regression target is a normalized reporter-expression readout over an ionizable-lipid library (the AGILE / Xu et al. set), distributed on a roughly log-scaled range of about [−2.3, 16]. The learning problem is structure→activity regression under a fixed featurization.

The paper's thesis is provocative: classical molecular representations paired with shallow models rival a purpose-built pretrained graph neural network (AGILE) on the refined dataset — implying the expensive representation learning buys little here. Our job is to test that claim independently, then probe its generalization.

What we're predicting: a single number per molecule — how well its fat bubble delivers mRNA into cells. Every model in this project is a machine that guesses that number from the molecule's structure.
Part 02 · The Key Idea

Turning a molecule into numbers

Computers can't read molecules — only numbers. So step one is translating each molecule into a list of numbers that describes it. This is called featurization.

High School

Chemists write a molecule as a short text code called a SMILES string. Our program reads that code and produces 2,258 numbers that describe the molecule, using two "lenses":

• A fingerprint (2,048 numbers): a checklist of little building-block patterns — "does this molecule contain this shape, and how many times?"
• A nutrition label (210 numbers): overall properties like weight, greasiness, and number of rings.

Glue them together → 2,258 numbers per molecule. That's what every model actually learns from.

Undergrad

Each SMILES is mapped to two feature blocks. Block 1: a 2,048-dimensional count Morgan (circular) fingerprint — it hashes each atom's local substructure environment into bins and counts occurrences. Block 2: 210 RDKit physicochemical descriptors (molecular weight, LogP, ring counts, topological indices…).

Concatenating gives a fixed 2,258-dimensional input vector, shared identically across all four models so comparisons are apples-to-apples.

PhD

X = [ count-ECFP(2048) ‖ RDKitDescriptors(210) ] ∈ ℝ2258. The fingerprint is a hashed count vector (not binary) with chirality enabled; descriptors are DeepChem's RDKitDescriptors set in alphabetical order, with Ipc computed as its average variant to avoid numerical blow-up.

Prior to modeling, features and target are jointly scaled with a single MinMaxScaler to [−1, 1] over all rows — a procedure we reproduce exactly (including its mild leakage; see the Reproduce section) for comparability.

Part 03 · The Hardest Part

Cracking the feature recipe

The authors gave us their final numbers as an "answer key," but not a clear recipe. We had to reverse-engineer our own recipe until our numbers matched theirs exactly. Two mysteries stood in the way.

Mystery #1 — the fingerprint that was 8× too small

High School

Our first fingerprint found about 56 patterns per molecule. Theirs found about 420 — nearly 8 times more! We dug into their code and found the reason: their setting looked at patterns of every size, not just tiny ones. Once we matched that, our fingerprints matched theirs almost perfectly.

Undergrad

Reproducing the fingerprint meant matching DeepChem's CircularFingerprint(radius=1024, counts, chiral). That radius is effectively unbounded — it enumerates substructure environments of every size — which is why ~420 bins are populated versus ~56 at the textbook radius 2. After the fix: 1,052 / 1,100 molecules matched exactly.

PhD

The first positional argument to DeepChem's CircularFingerprint is the Morgan radius; 1024 saturates environment enumeration, densifying the folded 2,048-vector. Our exact-match audit isolated the 48 non-matching molecules as pure version-dependent hash reshuffling — total counts invariant, max per-bin |Δ| = 3 — not a semantic difference.

Mystery #2 — which 210 descriptors, in what order?

High School

Our chemistry software offered 217 possible properties, but the answer key used exactly 210. Which 7 were dropped, and in what order? We lined them up one by one and discovered the authors used a slightly older software version missing 7 newer properties. After matching that, 209 of 210 matched exactly — the last one is off by a rounding-level amount.

Undergrad

The 210 descriptors are DeepChem's set in alphabetical order: the current 217 minus 7 newer RDKit additions, with Ipc set to its average variant. Result: 209/210 columns exact. The lone holdout, NumHAcceptors, differs by ≤1 on some molecules (r = 0.93) due to a definitional change between RDKit versions.

PhD

Column alignment across all 1,100 rows recovered the exact descriptor list and ordering. The residual discrepancy is a NumHAcceptors definitional drift (|Δ| ≤ 1, r = 0.93) that cannot be undone without pinning the authors' exact RDKit build. Post-scaling, one of 210 features off by ≤1 is negligible — confirmed by the matching baseline scores.

Part 04 · The Contestants

Four models, simplest to fanciest

All four see the same 2,258 numbers. They differ in how they turn those numbers into a prediction.

kNN simplest

To predict a new molecule, find the 5 most similar molecules it already knows and average their scores. It doesn't really "learn" — it looks up look-alikes. Remember this one — it wins later.

k-Nearest Neighbors (k = 5, uniform weights): prediction = mean of the 5 closest training points in feature space. A local, non-parametric baseline.

A local constant estimator; no training beyond indexing. Its inductive bias is smoothness in the chosen metric — exactly what makes it robust under distribution shift.

Random Forest middle

Builds 100 little yes/no "flowcharts" (decision trees) and averages their votes.

An ensemble of 100 regression trees (bagging + feature subsampling), predictions averaged. Handles nonlinearity and feature interactions.

Bootstrap-aggregated CART regressors, squared-error criterion. Variance reduction via decorrelated trees; limited extrapolation beyond the training envelope.

SVR middle

Fits a smooth curved surface through the data and reads predictions off it.

Support Vector Regression with an RBF kernel: fits a smooth nonlinear function using support vectors and an ε-insensitive loss.

Kernel estimator in an RBF-induced RKHS; deterministic given data, hence zero seed variance — useful as a pipeline-correctness check.

MLP the star

A neural network — a learning machine with 7 stages. It guesses, sees how wrong it was, and nudges itself to improve, thousands of times. This is the paper's best model.

A 7-layer feedforward net (2258→200→300→500→500→300→200→1), ReLU activations, Adam optimizer, MSE loss, ~100 epochs with best-validation-epoch selection.

MLP, lr 2e-4, batch 100; "early stopping" = restoring argmin-val-loss weights over a full 100-epoch run. Highest in-distribution capacity, but most exposed to covariate shift (shown later).

Part 05 · Did It Reproduce?

The headline result — matched

We trained the star model on the same data split the authors used and compared all four grades. Because a neural network varies run-to-run, we trained it 10 times and averaged.

High School

The score is called R²: 1.0 is a perfect predictor, 0.0 is useless. Our model scored 0.812; the paper reported 0.816. The tiny gap is just the model's normal run-to-run luck — basically the same score. It reproduced.

Undergrad

Over 10 seeds the MLP reached R² = 0.8123 ± 0.0107 versus the paper's 0.8161 — well within one standard deviation. RMSE, MAE and Pearson r all bracket the reported values too. The deterministic baselines (kNN, SVR) match to ~0.01, isolating our pipeline from theirs.

PhD

All four metrics contain the reported values within seed variance (σR² ≈ 0.011). Single-seed 0.799 vs 10-seed 0.812 quantifies initialization/minibatch stochasticity. SVR matching to ~1e-2 rules out featurization or preprocessing divergence as a confound.

Part 06 · The Stress Test · our contribution

What happens on molecules it's never seen?

The random test was a bit of an "open-book exam" — test molecules often had close look-alikes in the study set. So we ran a harder, "closed-book" test: hide entire structural families, so the test molecules are genuinely new shapes.

High School

A scaffold is a molecule's core skeleton. In a scaffold split, whole families with the same skeleton go only into the exam — so the model faces brand-new shapes. The result below is dramatic: the fancy model's score falls from 0.81 to 0.49, and the simplest model (kNN) becomes the best.

Undergrad

Under a Murcko scaffold split (an out-of-distribution test), the MLP collapses 0.81 → 0.49, the model ranking inverts to kNN > MLP > RF > SVR, and — not reported in the paper — the MLP's seed variance inflates 5×. kNN barely moves (Δ ≈ 0.02).

PhD

Bemis–Murcko scaffold partitioning induces covariate shift between train and test. Flexible learners overfit the training manifold and degrade most; kNN, as a local estimator, degrades gracefully. Variance inflation (σ 0.011 → 0.055) signals unstable optima under shift. Implication: random-split model selection is anti-correlated with prospective, novel-scaffold utility.

Part 07 · The Bottom Line

What it all means

High School

Did it reproduce? Yes — we rebuilt the paper's best model from scratch and got the same score. The claim is real and repeatable.

The catch we found: that great score comes from an "open-book" test. On a fair "closed-book" test with new molecule shapes, the fancy model loses nearly half its skill and gets unreliable — while the simplest method holds up best. So for designing genuinely new medicines, don't trust the fancy model just because it won the easy test.

Undergrad

Reproduced: from-scratch features + training code, on the authors' exact splits, give R² = 0.812 vs their 0.816 — statistically identical.

Generalization caveat: the headline is an in-distribution number. Under a scaffold split the MLP drops to ~0.49 with 5× variance, and kNN becomes both the best and most stable model. Model selection on a random split is misleading for prospective design; kNN is the honest baseline any model must beat.

PhD

Reproducibility: the headline MLP result and all baselines reproduce within seed variance on identical partitions, using an independently reconstructed featurization validated to the answer key — so the paper's central "shallow beats pretrained GNN" claim holds.

Robustness contribution: we add a scaffold-split analysis with 10-seed statistics showing (i) mean collapse, (ii) ranking inversion, and (iii) variance inflation for the MLP. Together these argue that in-distribution leaderboards mis-rank models for out-of-distribution lipid design, and that a local estimator (kNN) is a stronger, more stable prior under scaffold shift.

Appendix

Glossary

Every technical term, in one place.

Lipid nanoparticle
A tiny fat bubble that delivers mRNA into cells.
Transfection efficiency
How well an LNP delivers its cargo — the number we predict.
SMILES
A compact text string encoding a molecule's structure.
Featurization
Turning a molecule into a list of numbers a computer can learn from.
Morgan fingerprint
2,048 numbers counting which structural patterns a molecule contains.
RDKit descriptors
210 measured properties (weight, greasiness, ring counts…).
Radius
How big a neighborhood the fingerprint inspects. The authors used "unlimited," not the usual 2.
Split
Dividing data into training / validation / test groups.
Scaffold
A molecule's core skeleton; molecules sharing one are structural cousins.
Scaffold split
A hard, realistic split where the test set has brand-new skeletons.
kNN
Simplest model: average the 5 most similar known molecules. Won the hard test.
Random Forest
Averages the votes of 100 small decision trees.
SVR
Fits a smooth curved surface through the data.
MLP / neural network
A layered learning machine; the paper's best model.
Epoch
One full pass of the model through the training data.
Early stopping
Keeping the model version that scored best on the validation quiz.
R²
Main grade: 1.0 perfect, 0.0 useless.
RMSE / MAE
Average prediction error (RMSE punishes big misses more). Lower is better.
Pearson r
How well predictions and truth move together (1.0 = perfect).
Standard deviation
How much a number bounces between runs — a consistency measure.
Data leak
Letting test info influence setup, inflating scores. We copied the authors' mild one for fairness and disclosed it.
AGILE
A heavyweight pretrained rival model; scored 0.27. Used as a fixed reference.
Out-of-distribution
Test data unlike the training data — the realistic hard case.
